How Much is Buying This Thing Going to Delay My Retirement?

The Old Way: How Many Hot ‘n Spicy McChickens* is This Gonna Cost Me?

Whenever I see something shiny and fun that I want to buy, I try to figure out a way to make the cost more tangible so I can determine whether or not it’s worth it. In high school and college I used to think of the opportunity cost in terms of the other things I would likely buy with the money instead. For instance, how many Chipotle burritos is this diamond ring going to cost me? 125? That’s a lot of burritos... maybe I’d rather have the burritos instead of getting married.
After I graduated and got a real job that paid me more money than I was comfortable with spending, I started investing any money that I didn’t spend each month. I’ve been able to start putting money toward my retirement accounts as well as make some investments into taxable accounts for shorter term goals.
Pretty great, huh? Sure… but this completely ruined my previous opportunity cost strategy. If I didn’t buy an object of my desire, neglecting the purchase didn’t equate to losing X burritos, instead it just came out of the money that I would have invested at the end of the month. Whatever dude…
But wait, isn’t that a huge deal?? If invested money grows steadily over time and I have a fairly long time horizon, I could be losing out on way more than a few burritos just by buying this thing. Like fancy cars or mansions or the opportunity to help my (hopefully) future kids through college. Is that slice of pizza really worth not being able to hire a professional chef for the last 30 years of my life? Nah bro.
Time for a new strategy. Instead of things, let's focus on time. Time to retirement, to be specific. After “retirement”, I can spend the rest of my life tinkering with pet projects, building Hogwarts in Minecraft, or doing whatever I feel like I want to do, gosh. So that’s the million dollar question: If instead of buying this thing, I invest the money, how much earlier can I retire?

The New Way: Middle School Math

How much money do you need to retire? Here is one simple way to think about it:
We’ll define “retirement” as the moment you acquire enough wealth to live off of interest from your investments forever. That means you make all the money you need to spend, without working yourself by getting your money to work for you. It won’t matter if you live for 6 more years or 600 more years.
To calculate how much wealth you need to accumulate, with some assumptions, it only takes two steps:
  1. Determine your annual expenses (food, rent/mortgage, fun things, child care, etc…). One assumption is that you will continue to spend this much every year in retirement.
  2. Multiply the number you calculated in (1) by 25. This is a guideline for how much you need to retire.
That’s it. If you spend $40,000 per year you should save $1,000,000 before retiring.
Woah, what about inflation and stuff? Where did that “25” number come from? The 25 assumes that the market will grow at 7% percent per year (over the long term), and the dollar will inflate at a rate of 3% per year (again, over the long term). That means that you can expect to withdraw 4% of your nest egg each year while maintaining the balance of your account.
If you spend $35,000 (2017 dollars) per year, you need $875,000 (2017 dollars) to retire. You can withdraw $35,000 (2017 dollars), or 4% of the $875,000, per year and expect that you will accrue interest/dividends/capital gains fast enough that this account will replenish itself indefinitely. In other words, the value of the account, on average ** , will never decrease over time.

And keeping your money in a savings account generally means that you are effectively losing money to inflation (even though the dollar amount continues to rise, things become more expensive so you can actually buy less)... so you’ll never retire. When you consider investing in the stock market as a whole (index funds, ETFs), over long time horizons, it’s a lot less risky than it might seem.
